Krishna Janmashtami Festival

Events for Culture and Society Assignment by Klaudia, Imesha and Temi


Celebration, Rituals and Tradition

Written by Imesha

To give an significant value, the festival is primarily celebrated in Mathura and Vrindavan (Brindaban), the locations of Krishna's childhood and early adolescence. He is traditionally born at midnight, therefore believers observe a vigil and fast the day before while following the proper dietary guidelines to guarantee a fulfilling and healthy birthday celebration for Lord Krishna. In this way, it adds an exceptional rites of purification to this event festival, as in this custom, participants are properly cleansed (Andrews and Leopold, 2013).

Krishna is then worshipped after being washed in water, milk, and honey and clothed in new garments. Sweetmeats are first dedicated to the deity and then given to all family members as prasada, or the god's leftovers that convey his favour. In addition, it follows the rites of exchange in this time of giving as it is donated food and water to needy people to cherish the genuine spirit of Krishna Janmashtami. Besides, temples and domestic shrines are adorned with leaves and flowers.


 
 
 

Krishna devotees commemorate his birth by creating elaborate representations of Mathura, where he was born, the Yamuna River, which he was transported to safety, and Gokul (ancient Vraja), the scene of his childhood, with small images of the god, the other participants, and the animals and birds of the forest. In the streets, milk pots are suspended from tall poles. People participate in this activity in which an earthen pot or matki is tied high above the ground because Sri Krishna enjoyed eating butter. To shatter the matki, individuals sometimes construct a human pyramid (Chakrabarti and Chatterjee, 2020). As it involves the concept of 'adaptation' to display the authentic festival, it is slightly developed while preserving traditions (Laing and Frost, 2015). As, this custom makes an appealing and engaging activity for the participants. This is reminiscent of Krishna's boyhood game with the cowherd boys, in which they stole the curds that were hanging out of their mothers' grasp. In addition, there are group singing and dancing during the festival. Hindu devotional singing known as Kirtan is also practised, and worshippers take part in dance-dramas that portray the life of Lord Krishna (Zeidan, 2023).
